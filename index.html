OSCE Model Answers

Station 1 — Ear Furuncle

Diagnosis
Recurrent furunculosis of the right external auditory canal (caused by Staph. aureus)
Why pain on chewing?
The TMJ is directly adjacent to the cartilaginous EAC — jaw movements transmit pressure onto the furuncle, intensifying pain.
Key Investigation
Blood glucose analysis — to exclude/detect diabetes mellitus (risk factor for recurrent furunculosis)
Treatment
Medical: Systemic antibiotics (anti-staphylococcal) + analgesics. Local: glycerine ichthyol ointment or antibiotic cream. Control diabetes if found.
Surgical: NEVER incise or excise a furuncle in the EAC (risk of perichondritis). If pointing, gentle expression only.
⚠️ High-yield: Never incise EAC furuncle — risk of perichondritis and cartilage necrosis!

Station 2 — Malignant Otitis Externa

Diagnosis
Malignant otitis externa (Necrotizing OE / Skull base osteomyelitis) — caused by Pseudomonas aeruginosa
Investigations
1. Culture + sensitivity (from EAC) — confirms Pseudomonas. 2. HbA1c. 3. CT scan of temporal bone. 4. Bone scan (Gallium/Tc-99). 5. Biopsy to exclude tumour.
Treatment
Medical: Hospitalisation + control of diabetes + IV fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin) + local EAC cleaning by suction + local antibiotic drops.
Surgical: Debridement if needed.
Complications
Facial palsy (CN VII) · Lower cranial nerve palsies (IX, X, XI, XII) · Parotid swelling · Trigeminal facial pain

Station 3 — Acute Suppurative Otitis Media

Diagnosis
Acute suppurative otitis media (ASOM) — bilateral
Otoscopic Findings (Suppurative Stage)
Bulging, angry red drum · Loss of luster · Loss of landmarks (handle of malleus, cone of light) · Bulging (not yet perforated)
Treatment
1. Analgesics + antipyretics
2. Antibiotics (Amoxicillin 1st line, or Amoxicillin/Clavulanate)
3. Nasal decongestants (to open ET)
4. Myringotomy — if bulging drum + no improvement after 48h, or complications

Station 4 — Acute Mastoiditis

Diagnosis
Left acute mastoiditis (complication of AOM)
CT Findings
1. Clouding/opacity of mastoid air cells · 2. Erosion of mastoid air cell septa
Clinical Tenderness
Cymba conchae (Mc Ewen's triangle) · Mastoid tip
Treatment
Medical: Hospitalisation + IV antibiotics + analgesics + local drainage (myringotomy)
Surgical: Cortical mastoidectomy if no response to medical treatment after 48h, or if complications develop
Complications
Lateral sinus thrombosis · Labyrinthitis · Facial palsy · Petrositis · Meningitis · Brain abscess

Station 5 — Ramsay Hunt Syndrome

Diagnosis
Herpes zoster oticus (Ramsay Hunt Syndrome) — reactivation of VZV at the geniculate ganglion
Electrophysiological Tests of Facial Nerve
1. NET — Nerve Excitability Test (after 3 days) · 2. ENoG — Electroneurography (after 3 days) · 3. EMG — Electromyography (after 3 weeks)
Treatment
1. Antivirals — Acyclovir 800mg × 5 times/day × 10 days
2. Corticosteroids — Prednisolone 10mg × 3 weeks
3. Analgesics (Paracetamol)
4. Eye care (artificial tears, eye patch at night)
5. Antibiotics to prevent secondary infection
⚠️ The triad: severe otalgia + facial palsy + vesicles in the EAC = Ramsay Hunt

Station 6 — Perichondritis

Diagnosis
Perichondritis of the auricle (after insect sting)
Treatment
Medical: Systemic + local antibiotics · Analgesics
Surgical: Incision + drainage with removal of necrotic cartilage if pus/abscess forms · Tight bandage applied
Complication
Necrosis of cartilage → fibrosis → cauliflower ear
Function of Auricle
Collection and localisation of sound

Station 7 — Hematoma of Auricle

Diagnosis
Hematoma of the auricle ("Boxer's ear") — blood extravasation between perichondrium and cartilage
Treatment
1. Drainage (incision + evacuation of blood)
2. Application of tight/pressure bandage
3. Systemic antibiotics to prevent secondary infection
Complications (if untreated)
1. Perichondritis · 2. Cauliflower ear (cartilage necrosis + fibrosis)

Station 8 — Otitic Barotrauma

Otoscopy Finding
Air-fluid level (haemotympanum) — fluid/blood visible as a dark meniscus behind the tympanic membrane
Diagnosis
Otitic barotrauma (aviation barotrauma)
Mechanism
Eustachian tube blocked by cold → during rapid aircraft descent → atmospheric pressure ↑ while ME pressure ↓ → negative pressure in ME → effusion (± haemotympanum). Severe cases → TM rupture.
Treatment
Mild (SOM): 1. Nasal decongestants · 2. Valsalva manoeuvre (to open ET)
Moderate: 3. Myringotomy + drainage
Severe (ruptured drum): 4. Myringoplasty
⚠️ Flying with a cold is the classic risk factor — blocked ET = barotrauma

Station 9 — Referred Otalgia

4 Main Ear Symptoms
1. Hearing loss · 2. Vertigo · 3. Tinnitus · 4. Discharge (otorrhoea)
Normal TM + Otalgia = ?
Referred otalgia — pain originates outside the ear and is referred to the ear via shared sensory nerve supply
5 Causes of Referred Otalgia
1. Dental disease (TMJ dysfunction, dental caries) — CN V
2. Tonsillitis / peritonsillar abscess — CN IX
3. Cancer larynx / pyriform fossa — CN X (Arnold's)
4. Maxillary sinusitis — CN V
5. Cervical spine disease — C2/C3
4 Sensory Nerves Supplying the Ear
1. CN V (Trigeminal) — auriculotemporal nerve → upper 2/3 of EAC
2. CN VII (Facial) — posterior EAC (concha)
3. CN IX (Glossopharyngeal) — Jacobson's nerve → ME mucosa
4. CN X (Vagus) — Arnold's nerve → posterior 1/2 of EAC
Memory: 5-7-9-10 — all supply the ear, explaining why many head/neck diseases cause referred ear pain

Part Four

Quick Revision Notes

High-Yield Bullets
🦠
Ear Furuncle
  • Organism: Staph. aureus (localized OE)
  • Site: outer 1/3 of EAC (hair follicle)
  • Pain ↑ on jaw movement (TMJ adjacent)
  • Tragus pressure → pain
  • Rx: ABs + analgesics + glycerine ichthyol
  • NEVER incise → perichondritis risk
  • Always check blood glucose
💀
Malignant Otitis Externa
  • = Skull base osteomyelitis
  • Organism: Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Patient: elderly + uncontrolled DM
  • Granulation on floor of EAC = pathognomonic
  • Severe nocturnal otalgia — unresponsive to analgesics
  • Complication: Facial nerve palsy
  • Rx: IV Ciprofloxacin + debridement + DM control
  • CT negative → Bone scan next
👂
ASOM — Key Points
  • Most common organisms: Strep. pneumo, H. flu, Moraxella
  • NOT Pseudomonas in AOM
  • Suppurative stage: bulging red drum + throbbing pain at night
  • Pain relief → perforation → discharge
  • Rx: ABs + analgesics + nasal decongestants
  • Myringotomy: if bulging drum, no response after 48h
  • Risk factor in infants: bottle feeding
🔀
Referred Otalgia — Nerve Map
  • CN V → TMJ, sinusitis, salivary gland, dental
  • CN VII → Ramsay Hunt syndrome
  • CN IX (Jacobson) → tonsillitis, pharynx
  • CN X (Arnold) → larynx cancer, oesophagus
  • C2/C3 → cervical spine disease
  • Normal ear + otalgia = always think REFERRED
  • Referred otalgia ≠ allergic rhinitis
🌡️
Ramsay Hunt Syndrome
  • = Herpes Zoster Oticus
  • Triad: Otalgia + FN palsy + Vesicles in EAC
  • Virus: VZV — reactivated at geniculate ganglion
  • May cause SNHL + vertigo
  • Rx: Acyclovir + Prednisolone
  • Electrophysiology: NET (3d) → ENoG (3d) → EMG (3w)
✈️
Otitic Barotrauma
  • Occurs during DESCENT (NOT ascent)
  • Also: diving, blast injury
  • Risk: flying with a cold (blocked ET)
  • Mechanism: Atmospheric P ↑, ME P ↓ → negative ME pressure → effusion / haemotympanum
  • Otoscopy: air-fluid level (classic sign)
  • Rx mild: nasal decongestants + Valsalva
  • Rx moderate: myringotomy
  • No purulent discharge (differentiates from AOM)
🥊
Auricular Hematoma
  • "Boxer's ear" — blunt trauma
  • Blood between perichondrium and cartilage
  • Non-tender, fluctuant swelling
  • Rx: Incision + evacuation + pressure bandage + ABs
  • Complication: Cauliflower ear (perichondritis → fibrosis)
  • Never just aspirate without bandage — reaccumulates
🦟
Perichondritis
  • Inflammation of auricle cartilage/perichondrium
  • Causes: insect sting, trauma, burns, ear piercing
  • Painful, red, swollen pinna
  • Rx: systemic + local ABs + analgesics
  • Surgical if abscess: drain + remove necrotic cartilage
  • Complication: Cauliflower ear
  • Lobule NOT involved (no cartilage in lobule)
🦠
Otomycosis
  • Main symptom: ITCHING (not pain)
  • Organisms: Aspergillus niger (black), Candida
  • Otoscopy: white mass with black dots
  • Rx: local cleaning + antifungal drops (Nystatin) × 3 weeks
  • Systemic treatment NOT needed
  • Common in swimmers and after antibiotic ear drops
📋
Bullous Myringitis
  • Viral TM inflammation with blebs (bullae)
  • Ear pain ALWAYS present
  • Haemorrhagic blebs on TM and EAC
  • No facial palsy (differentiates from Ramsay Hunt)
  • Rx: analgesics + antiviral if needed
💥
Traumatic TM Rupture
  • Causes: slap, blast, ear wash, barotrauma
  • Otoscopy: right ear → handle of malleus on LEFT side of image
  • Heals spontaneously in 3 months
  • Rx: systemic ABs + nasal decongestants (open ET)
  • No ear drops (may enter ME)
  • Keep ear dry — avoid wetting
  • Myringoplasty if fails to heal after 3–6 months
⚡
High-Yield Exam Facts
  • Pain + DM + granulation = MOE until proven otherwise
  • Pain + vesicles + FN palsy = Ramsay Hunt
  • Itching + black spots + swimmer = Otomycosis
  • Pain + flight + cold history = Barotrauma
  • Pain + normal TM = Referred otalgia
  • Blunt trauma + non-tender swelling = Haematoma
  • Insect sting + red tender pinna = Perichondritis
📌 Final Note: The most commonly tested topics in this chapter are Malignant Otitis Externa, Ramsay Hunt Syndrome, Referred Otalgia nerve pathways, and ASOM stages. Focus on clinical scenarios — the diagnosis is usually hidden in the history. Good luck! 🎯
